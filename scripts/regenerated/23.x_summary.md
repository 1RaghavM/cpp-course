## Chapter 23 Summary

This chapter explored how objects relate to one another, from tight lifecycle coupling to fleeting one-call interactions. Understanding these relationships lets you choose the right coupling level for each design situation.

### Object relationship taxonomy

C++ recognizes four relationship types ranked by strength: composition (strongest), aggregation, association, and dependency (weakest). Each implies different ownership semantics, lifetime rules, and directionality.

### Composition

Composition models a "part-of" relationship where the part cannot exist independently of the whole. The whole creates, owns, and destroys its parts. In C++, composition is implemented with normal member variables. The part's lifetime is bounded by the whole's lifetime. Most class member variables are compositions.

### Aggregation

Aggregation models a "has-a" relationship where the whole references a part but does not manage its lifetime. A `Department` has `Teacher` members, but teachers exist independently. In C++, aggregation is typically implemented with pointers or references to externally-owned objects. The part can belong to multiple wholes simultaneously.

### Association

Association models a "uses-a" relationship with no ownership. Neither side manages the other's lifetime, and the relationship can be bidirectional. Associations are typically implemented with pointers and may involve each side holding a container of pointers to the other.

### Dependencies

A dependency is the weakest relationship: one object temporarily uses another, typically as a function parameter or locally constructed helper. The dependent does not store the dependency as a member.

### Container classes

Container classes hold and organize collections of other objects, handling storage, access, and iteration. `std::vector`, `std::array`, and `std::map` are standard containers. Writing your own container means managing a dynamic array, exposing element access, and tracking size. The container-element relationship is composition when the container owns copies, or aggregation when it holds pointers.

### std::initializer_list

`std::initializer_list<T>` lets a constructor accept a brace-enclosed list of values like `{1, 2, 3}`. Provide a constructor taking `std::initializer_list<T>` to let callers write `MyClass obj{a, b, c}`. The list is lightweight and should be passed by value. When both an `initializer_list` constructor and a matching regular constructor exist, the `initializer_list` version wins for brace initialization.
